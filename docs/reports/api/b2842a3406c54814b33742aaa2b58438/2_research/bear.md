### 1. The Valuation Trap: "Cheap" Is an Illusion of Math
You cite a Forward P/E of 18.5x and a PEG of 0.63 as evidence of value. That is the most dangerous metric in investing right now. Why? Because it relies on Forward EPS of $11.24—a number that requires revenue to *continue* compounding at 65%+ from a base of $216B. That is a $140B *incremental* revenue step-up that the market has already priced in.

If the AI build-out even *slows* to a "normal" growth rate, those forward estimates get slashed, and that "cheap" 18.5x forward P/E instantly becomes a stagnant 30x or 40x multiple on a stock that has stopped growing. A PEG ratio is only useful if the "G" is sustainable. With revenue growth decelerating from 126% to 66% in one year, the math is telling you the "G" is fading. You aren't paying for value; you're paying for perfection.

### 2. The Margin Moat Is a Cyclical Peak, Not a Floor
You laud the 75% gross margin as if it’s a permanent structural shift. It’s not; it’s a symptom of a supply shortage. The FQ1 margin dip to 60.5% wasn’t just a "blip"—it was a warning shot of what margins look like when product mixes normalize or customers push back on pricing. 

More importantly, you’re ignoring the competitive moat erosion. Why are hyperscalers like AMD and Broadcom surging? Because they are the avenues for custom silicon. The big cloud players—Microsoft, Amazon, Google—do not want to be beholden to NVDA’s 75% margins. They are developing their own ASICs. That $500M Corning deal you’re celebrating? That’s a drop in the bucket compared to the capex they are spending on *non-NVDA* infrastructure to break your monopoly. The "toll collector" is about to have a by-pass road built around it.
